College Terms & Acronyms

Master the language of college admissions with our comprehensive guide to essential terms, acronyms, and concepts you'll encounter throughout your college journey.

Application Terms

FAFSA

Free Application for Federal Student Aid

The federal form required to determine eligibility for federal student financial aid, including grants, loans, and work-study.

CSS

CSS Profile

An additional financial aid application used by colleges to distribute non-federal aid, including institutional scholarships and grants.

SAT

Scholastic Assessment Test

A standardized test used for college admissions that measures reading, writing, and math skills.

ACT

American College Testing

A standardized college entrance exam that assesses English, Math, Reading, and Science. Also accepted by most colleges as an SAT alternative.

AP

Advanced Placement

College-level courses offered in high school. Passing the AP exam may earn college credit or advanced placement.

IB

International Baccalaureate

An international curriculum offering challenging courses and exams. Strong performance can lead to college credit.

Personal Statement

An essay (usually 500-650 words) submitted with your application where you tell your story and explain why you're a good fit for a college.

Supplemental Essays

Additional short essays required by some colleges that ask specific questions about why you want to attend or about your interests.

Letter of Recommendation

Written evaluation from a teacher, counselor, or mentor that speaks to your character, abilities, and potential as a college student.

Transcript

Official record of all your courses, grades, and GPA. Required with every college application.

GPA

Grade Point Average

Numerical average of all your grades, typically on a 4.0 scale. A major factor in college admissions decisions.

Early Decision

Binding agreement to attend a college if accepted. You commit to enroll and withdraw other applications. Must be submitted by early November.

Admission Types & Deadlines

Early Action

Non-binding early application option. You receive a decision early but are not committed to attending. Allows you to compare options.

Regular Decision

Standard application timeline with admission decisions typically sent in March-April. Not binding; allows you to compare all acceptances before deciding.

Rolling Admission

Colleges review applications as they arrive and send decisions throughout the year. Earlier submission typically increases acceptance chances.

EDI

Early Decision Round 1

The first round of binding early decision applications, typically due November 1st or 15th.

EDII

Early Decision Round 2

A second binding early decision option for some colleges, typically due January 1st.

Binding Decision

A legal commitment to attend the college if accepted. Common with Early Decision. You must enroll if accepted.

Waitlist

When a college neither accepts nor rejects you, but places you on a list of potential students they may admit later if space opens up.

Deferral

When an early application decision is postponed to the regular decision round. Your application will be reconsidered later.

Financial Aid Terms

Merit Scholarship

Financial award based on academic achievement, test scores, or other accomplishments. Doesn't need to be repaid.

Need-Based Aid

Financial assistance given based on your family's demonstrated financial need as determined by the FAFSA and CSS Profile.

FERPA

Family Education Rights and Privacy Act

Federal law that protects the privacy of student education records. Gives students access to their records at age 18.

Federal Grant

Free money from the federal government that doesn't need to be repaid. Based on financial need.

Federal Loan

Borrowed money from the federal government that must be repaid with interest. Includes subsidized and unsubsidized loans.

Work-Study

Part-time on-campus or off-campus job available to students with financial need. Combines work and financial aid.

EFC/SAI

Expected Family Contribution / Student Aid Index

The amount a family is expected to contribute toward college costs based on FAFSA information. Used to determine financial aid.

Financial Aid Package

The total amount of financial aid (grants, scholarships, loans, work-study) a college offers you.

Cost of Attendance (COA)

The total estimated cost of attending a college for one year, including tuition, fees, room, board, and other expenses.

Full Ride

A scholarship that covers all costs of attendance, including tuition, fees, room, and board.

PLUS

Parent PLUS Loan

Federal loan available to parents of dependent students to help pay education costs. Parents are responsible for repayment.

Net Price

The actual price you pay to attend college after subtracting grants and scholarships from the total cost of attendance.

Academic Terms

Major

Your primary field of study in college. You choose a subject to focus on and take most of your courses in that area.

Minor

A secondary field of study that complements your major. Requires fewer courses than a major.

Elective

A course you choose to take that isn't required for graduation. Gives you flexibility in your schedule.

Prerequisite

A course that must be completed before you can enroll in another course. Often required for advanced classes.

Credit Hours

Units that measure the amount of time spent in a course. Typically, 1 credit = 1 hour of instruction per week for a semester.

Semester

One half of the academic year, typically lasting 15 weeks. Most colleges have a fall and spring semester.

Trimester

System that divides the year into three equal academic terms instead of two semesters.

Quarter System

Academic calendar dividing the year into four quarters of about 10 weeks each.

CLEP

College-Level Examination Program

Standardized tests that allow you to earn college credit by exam without taking the course.

Internship

Hands-on work experience in your field of study, usually during summer or while in school. Can be paid or unpaid.

Undergraduate

A student pursuing a bachelor's degree, typically for 4 years. Also refers to students in their first four years of college.

Graduate

A student pursuing an advanced degree (master's, PhD) after completing their bachelor's degree.

Campus & Student Life

Residential College

A college where most students live on campus in dorms. Housing is often required, especially freshman year.

Commuter School

A college where most students live off-campus and commute to school. Common at urban universities.

Office Hours

Designated times when professors are available to meet with students to discuss coursework, grades, and other concerns.

Advising

Guidance from an academic advisor who helps you choose courses, plan your major, and stay on track for graduation.

Orientation

Program for new students before the semester begins that introduces campus, meets other students, and covers college policies.

Sorority/Fraternity

Social organizations for students, usually Greek letter organizations. Offer community, networking, and social activities.

Club/Organization

Student groups focused on interests, hobbies, or activities. Allow you to connect with others and develop leadership skills.

Dean of Students

Administrative official responsible for student welfare, discipline, and support services on campus.

Registrar

Office responsible for managing academic records, registration, transcripts, and degree audit information.

Bursar

The office responsible for billing students, processing payments, and managing accounts and financial records.

Career Services

Department that helps students with career planning, job searches, internships, and professional development.

Academic Support

Services offered by colleges to help students succeed academically, including tutoring, writing centers, and study groups.

Institutional Terms

Public University

A university funded by state and federal government. Generally more affordable but larger than private institutions.

Private University

A university funded primarily by private sources like tuition and endowments. Usually smaller with higher tuition.

Liberal Arts College

Smaller college focused on broad education across many disciplines rather than specialization. Emphasizes critical thinking.

STEM

Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics. Fields that focus on quantitative and technical skills.

Endowment

Large fund of invested money donated to a college. Generated income is used for scholarships and operations.

Selectivity

How competitive a college is. Measured by acceptance rate, test scores, and GPA of admitted students.

Accreditation

Official recognition that a college meets quality standards set by education authorities. Important for degree validity.

Ivy League

Prestigious athletic conference of eight private universities known for academic excellence and selectivity.

Flagship

Flagship University

The primary public university in a state system, typically the oldest and most prestigious.

Community College

Two-year institution offering Associate degrees and vocational training. Affordable option for first two years before transfer.

Holistic Admissions

Admissions approach that considers the whole studentâ€”grades, test scores, essays, activities, background, and circumstances.

Test-Optional

Policy allowing students to apply without submitting standardized test scores. Gives flexibility in application process.

Degrees & Requirements

BA

Bachelor of Arts

Four-year degree emphasizing liberal arts and humanities. Provides broad education across multiple disciplines.

BS

Bachelor of Science

Four-year degree with focus on science, technology, engineering, or mathematics. More specialized than BA.

BFA

Bachelor of Fine Arts

Four-year degree in visual or performing arts. Combines creative training with academic coursework.

AA

Associate of Arts

Two-year degree in liberal arts and humanities. Often completed at community college before transferring to university.

AS

Associate of Science

Two-year degree focused on science and technical fields. Prepares students for careers or further education.

General Education

Required core courses all students must take regardless of major. Typically include writing, math, science, and humanities.

Core Curriculum

Set of required courses designed to provide foundational knowledge in major subject areas and develop critical thinking.

Degree Audit

Report showing progress toward graduation requirements. Lists completed courses and remaining requirements.

Transfer Credit

Credits earned at one institution that are accepted at another college toward degree requirements.

Satisfactory Academic Progress

Standard requiring students maintain minimum GPA and course load. Must be met to remain eligible for financial aid.

Academic Probation

Status when GPA falls below acceptable level. Student has set time to improve before dismissal.

Dean's List

Honor recognition for students achieving high GPA in a semester, typically 3.5 or higher. Notable achievement for resume.

Additional Important Terms

Yield

Percentage of admitted students who choose to enroll at a college. Shows how attractive accepted offers are.

Legacy

Preference given to applicants whose parents or relatives attended the college. Can impact admissions decisions.

First-Generation Student

Student whose parents did not attend a four-year college. Some colleges offer additional support for first-gen students.

Campus Visit

Tour of college facilities and sometimes meetings with admissions counselors. Shows demonstrated interest in application.

Demonstrated Interest

Actions showing genuine interest in a college (campus visit, email, application, attendance at info sessions). Factors into some admissions decisions.

Yield Protection

Practice by some colleges of rejecting applicants they believe won't enroll if accepted to protect their yield rate.

Gap Year

Taking time off between high school and college to work, travel, intern, or pursue personal interests. Then enrolling in college.

Articulation Agreement

Agreement between two colleges outlining how credits will transfer and which requirements are satisfied by transferred coursework.

Scattergram

Chart showing the relationship between test scores/GPA and college admissions outcomes. Helps predict acceptance chances.

Acceptance Rate

Percentage of applicants admitted to a college. Lower rates indicate more selective institutions.

Application Fee

Non-refundable fee required to submit a college application, typically $50-$75. Some colleges waive fees for financial need.

Application Portal

Online system where you submit your application, essays, and other required documents to a college.
